We scanned 50 GitHub repos using OpenAI — 94% would fail an EU AI Act audit

The EU AI Act is now partially enforceable. We ran our compliance scanner on 50 public GitHub repositories using popular AI frameworks. The results are sobering:

  • 94% had zero compliance documentation
  • 72% couldn't identify their AI risk classification
  • 0% had a Model Card or system description as required by Article 52

This isn't hypothetical. The first enforcement wave targets general-purpose AI systems (Article 53) — that includes any app calling GPT-4, Claude, or Mistral APIs.

The Stakes Are Real

Under the EU AI Act:

  • Non-compliance penalties reach up to €35M or 7% of global annual turnover
  • Enforcement starts in phases: high-risk systems in 2025, general-purpose AI in 2026
  • The first wave will focus on companies shipping AI in the EU — startups and scale-ups using OpenAI, Anthropic, or HuggingFace

If your app uses any of these frameworks and has EU users, you're in scope.

What the EU AI Act Requires

You don't need to be a compliance expert. Here are the three things you need to do:

1. Classify Your Risk Level

Is your AI system:

  • Unacceptable risk? (e.g., social credit scoring) — BANNED
  • High risk? (e.g., hiring, credit decisions, law enforcement) — Heavy compliance burden
  • Limited risk? (e.g., chatbots, deepfake detection) — Light transparency requirements
  • Minimal risk? (e.g., spam filtering, autocomplete) — Minimal requirements

Most apps using GPT-4 fall into limited or high risk. You need to document which.

2. Document Your System

You need a Model Card (standardized description) that includes:

  • What the AI system does
  • What data it was trained on
  • Known limitations and bias
  • How it's monitored post-deployment
  • Who can access and audit it

This doesn't need to be 100 pages. A GitHub issue or README section works fine as a starting point.

3. Maintain Ongoing Monitoring

You need to track:

  • How often your AI system fails or behaves unexpectedly
  • User complaints or edge cases
  • Changes in regulations (they're evolving)

Why This Matters for Startups

If you're a startup in the EU using AI:

  • You're required to comply. Not "encouraged" — required.
  • The law is already partially enforceable. Articles 51-58 (transparency, record-keeping) apply now.
  • Ignorance isn't a defense. Regulators will fine first, ask questions later.
  • You can't hide by being small. The 7% global turnover penalty applies to everyone.

The Good News

Compliance doesn't require hiring lawyers or spending 6 months on paperwork. You can:

  1. Classify your system yourself — It's a judgment call, not rocket science
  2. Write a Model Card — GitHub Wiki + checklist takes 2 hours
  3. Set up basic monitoring — Logs + alerts, no special tools needed

But you need to do it before enforcement tightens in 2026.
